**FSL exists to protect the integrity of the HDK App protocol** — the
coordination point that lets an App written for one harness run on any
harness. Without that protection, the protocol is forkable, the ecosystem
fragments, and the App you wrote stops working consistently across the
runtimes your users actually have installed.

This page explains what that means concretely and why standard FSL fits.

## The protocol is the coordination point

Every HDK App declares against a specific App protocol — the bytes-locked
intro, catalog format, tool-selection rule, and boundary marker in
`packages/rig/src/protocol.ts`. The runtime renders that protocol into the
spine. The model's behavior depends on those exact bytes.

When you publish an App for HDK, you're publishing against that protocol.
Your App's reliability depends on every HDK runtime — every harness, every
deployment, every version your users install — agreeing on the same
protocol. The protocol is what makes "I built an HDK App" a portable,
trustworthy statement.

## Protocol fragmentation is the threat

If the runtime ships under a permissive license without protection, a
well-resourced party can fork it, modify the protocol surface, and
distribute their variant under a different name. The mechanics are
straightforward:

1. Fork the open-source HDK runtime.
2. Modify the catalog format, tool-selection rule, or lifecycle semantics
   — small changes are enough.
3. Bundle it with captive distribution (a cloud vendor's managed offering,
   a vertically integrated stack, an "enterprise" build).
4. App developers now face a choice: target the upstream HDK App protocol,
   target the fork's protocol, build twice, or pick the bigger
   distribution.

This is not theoretical. It's exactly the pattern that played out with
Node.js / io.js (2014-2015, reconciled after a year of ecosystem
confusion), with OpenJDK vs vendor JDKs (subtle TLS / GC differences for
years), with Docker → Moby + runtime-spec splits, and more recently with
MCP variants shipping from multiple vendors. Each time, the underlying
issue was the same: a coordination protocol under a permissive license
with no defense against fragmentation.

**The cost is paid by App developers**, not by the entity that forked. App
developers have to test against multiple variants, support divergent
behaviors, or accept that their App only works on the bigger of the two
contracts. The fragmentation tax is distributed across the people who
trusted the protocol.

FSL's restriction is shaped precisely to block this: redistributors can't
ship a competing runtime that fragments the protocol. They can build
anything *on top of* HDK; they can't ship a parallel variant *of* HDK.

## Why FSL specifically

Several licenses could block protocol fragmentation. We chose
FSL-1.1-Apache-2.0 for four reasons:

**1. It converts to Apache 2.0 in two years.** The restriction has a
written expiration. Each released version of HDK becomes Apache 2.0
licensed exactly two years after its release date. The protection is for
the formative period when ecosystem coherence is most fragile; after that
window, the conversion is contractual, not a promise. If you care about
eventually-open commitments — and you should — FSL is one of the few
source-available licenses where that commitment is in the license text,
not in a blog post.

**2. The restriction is narrow.** FSL doesn't restrict commercial use,
redistribution, modification, embedding, or selling. Only Competing Use —
which we define as becoming a substitute or compatible-variant runtime.
Every other commercial scenario is permitted. See
[the FAQ](./faq) for the concrete list.

**3. It's standard, not bespoke.** FSL-1.1-Apache-2.0 is the canonical
text published by Sentry. Other Fair Source projects (Sentry, PowerSync,
Codecov) use it. License scanners increasingly recognize it. Procurement
orgs increasingly have a category for it. We could have written a custom
license tuned exactly to "protect the protocol" — and we considered it —
but a bespoke license forces every adopter into individual legal review.
The off-the-shelf license is the better trade.

**4. It's a recognized Fair Source license.** [Fair Source](https://fair.io)
is the classification for source-available licenses that explicitly
permit commercial use, free redistribution, and eventual open-source
conversion. The category is designed for exactly the situation HDK is in
— a coordination point that needs integrity protection during the
formative period without sacrificing developer-friendly access.

## What this means for you, the App developer

If you're building an HDK App, what FSL changes for you is approximately
nothing:

- **You can publish, sell, embed, and commercialize your App** as before
- **You can fork HDK, modify it, learn from it** as before
- **You can ship it inside any product, on any platform** as before
- **Your App's protocol will not be fragmented** by a forked runtime
  shipping a slightly-different variant during the period when
  fragmentation would cost you the most
- **The runtime your App targets will be Apache 2.0** within two years of
  each release — the long-term open-source guarantee is contractual

## Why not Apache 2.0 alone

Apache 2.0 is one of the strongest permissive licenses and has been right
for HDK 2.x. With HDK 3.0 introducing installable Apps as the
architectural core, the calculus shifts: under Apache 2.0 the protocol is
forkable, and a fragmentation attack has no legal barrier. The price App
developers pay if the ecosystem splits is real, and once the split
happens it's hard to undo (the Node.js / io.js reconciliation took a year
of significant ecosystem effort). FSL is the targeted intervention to
prevent that specific outcome during the specific window when it would
hurt App developers most.

After two years, Apache 2.0 is exactly what every released version
becomes.

## Why not a custom license

We considered writing a license tailored to "protect the protocol" with
maximum permissiveness on every other axis. The standard FSL is broader
than the strictly-needed restriction. The trade-off:

- A custom license fits the threat more surgically but is bespoke,
  requires individual legal review at every adopter, and has no
  recognition with license scanners or procurement systems.
- Standard FSL is slightly broader in scope but is recognized,
  documented, and defended by the Fair Source community.

The integration cost of bespoke licensing exceeded the marginal benefit
of a more surgical fit. We chose the standard.
